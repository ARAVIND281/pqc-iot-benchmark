\begin{abstract}
The imminent threat of quantum computing to classical cryptographic systems necessitates the adoption of post-quantum cryptography (PQC) across all computing domains. Resource-constrained Internet of Things (IoT) devices present unique challenges for PQC implementation due to severe limitations in memory, processing power, and energy availability. This paper presents a comprehensive empirical analysis of five NIST-standardized PQC algorithms---\kyber{}, \dilithium{}, \falcon{}, \sphincs{}, and \ntru{}---across three device classes defined by RFC 7228. Through over 45,000 benchmark measurements, we evaluate key generation, encapsulation/signing, and decapsulation/verification operations, along with memory footprint and energy consumption. Statistical analysis using ANOVA and Tukey HSD tests reveals significant performance differences (p < 0.001) between algorithms. We employ TOPSIS multi-criteria decision analysis to generate actionable recommendations for IoT developers. Our findings indicate that \kyber{}-512 and \dilithium{}-2 offer the best balance of security and performance for Class 1 and Class 2 devices, while Class 0 devices remain challenging for all current PQC implementations. We provide open-source benchmarking tools and datasets to facilitate reproducible research.
\end{abstract}

\begin{IEEEkeywords}
post-quantum cryptography, IoT, benchmarking, NIST standards, embedded systems, Kyber, Dilithium, FALCON, SPHINCS+

%-----------------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}
%-----------------------------------------------------------------------

The rapid advancement of quantum computing poses an existential threat to the cryptographic foundations of modern digital security. Shor's algorithm~\cite{shor1994algorithms} can factorize large integers and compute discrete logarithms in polynomial time, rendering RSA, DSA, and elliptic curve cryptography (ECC) vulnerable to attack by sufficiently powerful quantum computers. While the timeline for cryptographically relevant quantum computers (CRQCs) remains uncertain, the ``harvest now, decrypt later'' threat model compels immediate action for systems handling sensitive data with long-term confidentiality requirements~\cite{mosca2018cybersecurity}.

The Internet of Things (IoT) represents a particularly vulnerable domain in the post-quantum transition. With an estimated 75 billion connected devices projected by 2025~\cite{statista2021iot}, IoT systems pervade critical infrastructure including healthcare, industrial control, smart cities, and autonomous vehicles. Many IoT devices operate under severe resource constraints---limited memory (often <256 KB RAM), modest computational power (<100 MHz processors), and strict energy budgets (battery-powered with multi-year lifespans)~\cite{bormann2014coap}. These constraints create tension with post-quantum cryptographic algorithms, which typically demand significantly more resources than their classical predecessors.

In August 2024, the National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST) finalized the first set of post-quantum cryptographic standards~\cite{nist2024pqc}, including:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{ML-KEM} (\kyber{}): A lattice-based key encapsulation mechanism offering three security levels
    \item \textbf{ML-DSA} (\dilithium{}): A lattice-based digital signature scheme
    \item \textbf{SLH-DSA} (\sphincs{}): A hash-based stateless signature scheme
    \item \textbf{FN-DSA} (\falcon{}): A compact lattice-based signature scheme (expected 2025)
\end{itemize}

This paper addresses the critical research gap of quantifying PQC algorithm performance on resource-constrained IoT devices through rigorous empirical analysis. Our contributions include:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item A comprehensive benchmarking framework for evaluating PQC algorithms under realistic IoT constraints
    \item Empirical measurements of 14 algorithm parameter sets across 3 device classes (45,000+ data points)
    \item Statistical analysis identifying significant performance differences and correlations
    \item TOPSIS-based multi-criteria decision framework for algorithm selection
    \item Open-source tools and datasets for reproducible research
\end{enumerate}

%-----------------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Background and Related Work}
\label{sec:background}
%-----------------------------------------------------------------------

\subsection{Post-Quantum Cryptography Fundamentals}

Post-quantum cryptographic algorithms derive their security from mathematical problems believed to be resistant to both classical and quantum attacks. The primary families include:

\textbf{Lattice-based cryptography} relies on problems such as Learning With Errors (LWE) and its ring variant (RLWE). Lattice schemes offer excellent performance characteristics but require careful implementation to avoid side-channel vulnerabilities. \kyber{}~\cite{bos2018crystals} and \dilithium{}~\cite{ducas2018crystals} exemplify this family.

\textbf{Hash-based signatures} derive security from the collision resistance of hash functions, providing strong security guarantees under well-understood assumptions. \sphincs{}~\cite{bernstein2019sphincs} offers a stateless variant eliminating the state management challenges of earlier hash-based schemes.

\textbf{Code-based cryptography} and \textbf{multivariate cryptography} represent alternative approaches, though current NIST selections favor lattice-based schemes for key encapsulation and offer hash-based alternatives for signatures.

\subsection{IoT Device Classifications}

RFC 7228~\cite{bormann2014coap} establishes a taxonomy for constrained devices:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Class 0}: $\approx$10 KB RAM, highly constrained, typically offline
    \item \textbf{Class 1}: $\approx$50 KB RAM, capable of basic security protocols
    \item \textbf{Class 2}: $\approx$250 KB RAM, can support standard security stacks with optimization
\end{itemize}

Each class imposes distinct constraints on algorithm selection and implementation strategies.

\subsection{Related Work}

Several prior studies have examined PQC performance on embedded platforms. The pqm4 project~\cite{kannwischer2019pqm4} provides ARM Cortex-M4 implementations and cycle counts for NIST candidates. However, pqm4 focuses on optimized assembly implementations rather than realistic IoT deployment scenarios.

Bürstinghaus-Steinbach et al.~\cite{burstinghaus2020post} evaluated post-quantum TLS handshakes on embedded devices, finding significant latency increases compared to classical cryptography. Cheng et al.~\cite{cheng2021memory} analyzed memory consumption patterns, highlighting garbage collection challenges in resource-constrained environments.

Our work extends these efforts by providing:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Cross-algorithm comparison using unified methodology
    \item Device class-specific analysis aligned with RFC 7228
    \item Energy consumption modeling
    \item Statistical significance testing
    \item Multi-criteria decision analysis for practical deployment guidance
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Device Class Simulation}

We simulate three IoT device classes per RFC 7228 using process resource limits:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Class 0}: 10 KB RAM, 16 MHz equivalent, 0.1 mW idle power
    \item \textbf{Class 1}: 50 KB RAM, 48 MHz equivalent, 1.0 mW idle power
    \item \textbf{Class 2}: 250 KB RAM, 120 MHz equivalent, 10.0 mW idle power
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Benchmarking Protocol}

For each algorithm-device combination, we execute:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item 50 warmup iterations (discarded)
    \item 1,000 measured iterations per operation
    \item Operations: key generation, encapsulation/signing, decapsulation/verification
\end{enumerate}

Total measurements: 14 algorithms $\times$ 3 device classes $\times$ 3 operations $\times$ 1,000 iterations = 126,000 raw data points.

\subsection{Metrics}

We measure and derive the following metrics:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Execution time} (ms): Wall-clock time per operation
    \item \textbf{CPU cycles}: Derived from time and processor frequency
    \item \textbf{Peak memory} (KB): Maximum resident set size
    \item \textbf{Energy consumption} (mJ): Estimated using the model:
\end{itemize}

\begin{equation}
E = \frac{C \times \text{CPI} \times V \times I}{f}
\label{eq:energy}
\end{equation}

where $C$ is CPU cycles, CPI is cycles per instruction (assumed 1.5), $V$ is voltage, $I$ is current, and $f$ is clock frequency.

\subsection{Statistical Analysis}

We employ the following statistical methods:

\textbf{Data Cleaning}: Outlier detection using the interquartile range (IQR) method, removing values beyond $Q_1 - 1.5 \times \text{IQR}$ or $Q_3 + 1.5 \times \text{IQR}$.

\textbf{Normality Testing}: Shapiro-Wilk test applied to verify distributional assumptions.

\textbf{ANOVA}: One-way analysis of variance to test null hypotheses regarding equal means across algorithms.

\textbf{Post-hoc Analysis}: Tukey's Honestly Significant Difference (HSD) test for pairwise comparisons with family-wise error rate control.

\textbf{Correlation Analysis}: Pearson correlation coefficients between all metric pairs.

\subsection{TOPSIS Multi-Criteria Analysis}

For practical deployment recommendations, we apply the Technique for Order of Preference by Similarity to Ideal Solution (TOPSIS)~\cite{hwang1981multiple}. This method ranks alternatives based on geometric distance from ideal positive and negative solutions.

The decision matrix incorporates:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Execution time (minimize, weight: 0.30)
    \item Memory usage (minimize, weight: 0.25)
    \item Energy consumption (minimize, weight: 0.20)
    \item Key size (minimize, weight: 0.15)
    \item Security level (maximize, weight: 0.10)
\end{itemize}

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}
%-----------------------------------------------------------------------

\subsection{Practical Recommendations}

Based on our analysis, we offer the following recommendations:

\textbf{Class 2 devices} can deploy standard NIST PQC algorithms with minimal modification. \kyber{}-768 provides an excellent balance of Level 3 security and performance. For signatures, \dilithium{}-2 or \falcon{}-512 represent viable options.

\textbf{Class 1 devices} require careful algorithm selection. \kyber{}-512 fits within memory constraints while providing Level 1 security. \dilithium{}-2 remains feasible but leaves limited headroom for application logic.

\textbf{Class 0 devices} cannot support current PQC implementations directly. Possible mitigations include:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Delegating cryptographic operations to a gateway device
    \item Using symmetric-key protocols with pre-shared quantum-resistant keys
    \item Awaiting lightweight PQC variants from ongoing research
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Algorithm-Specific Insights}

\textbf{\kyber{}} emerges as the clear choice for key encapsulation on IoT devices. Its lattice structure enables efficient implementation, and all parameter sets outperform alternatives on constrained hardware.

\textbf{\dilithium{}} provides the most balanced signature scheme, with moderate key sizes and consistent performance. However, larger public keys compared to classical ECDSA impact storage requirements.

\textbf{\falcon{}}'s compact signatures (40\% smaller than \dilithium{}) come at the cost of complex Gaussian sampling, which proves challenging on resource-constrained processors.

\textbf{\sphincs{}} offers maximal security guarantees but imposes substantial performance penalties. We recommend \sphincs{} only for high-security applications on Class 2 devices where signing frequency is low.

\subsection{Limitations}

Our study has several limitations:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item Simulation-based benchmarking may not perfectly capture hardware behavior
    \item Energy estimates rely on theoretical models rather than direct measurement
    \item Side-channel resistance was not evaluated
    \item Results may vary across different PQC library implementations
\end{enumerate}

We identify several directions for future research:

\begin{itemize}
    \item Hardware-in-the-loop testing with physical IoT devices
    \item Investigation of hybrid classical/PQC schemes
    \item Analysis of PQC performance in multi-party protocols
    \item Development of lightweight PQC variants for Class 0 devices
    \item Side-channel analysis and countermeasures for embedded implementations
\end{itemize}

%-----------------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
%-----------------------------------------------------------------------

This paper presented a comprehensive empirical analysis of post-quantum cryptographic algorithms on resource-constrained IoT devices. Through over 45,000 benchmark measurements and rigorous statistical analysis, we demonstrated that:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item Lattice-based algorithms (\kyber{}, \dilithium{}) offer the best overall performance for IoT deployment
    \item Class 1 and Class 2 devices can support PQC with appropriate algorithm selection
    \item Class 0 devices require alternative approaches beyond direct PQC implementation
    \item TOPSIS multi-criteria analysis provides actionable deployment guidance
\end{enumerate}

As the cryptographic community transitions to post-quantum security, our findings and open-source tools contribute to evidence-based decision-making for IoT system architects and developers.
