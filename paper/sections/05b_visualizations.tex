\section{Extended Visualization and Data Aggregation}
\label{sec:visualizations}
%-----------------------------------------------------------------------

Tables report averages; distributions tell the real story. We generated 14 visualizations from the full 45,000-trace dataset to expose variance, correlation structure, and Pareto boundaries that summary statistics obscure.

\subsection{KEM Operation Scaling (Encapsulation and Decapsulation)}

While Figure \ref{fig:keygen_time} focused on initial derivation bounds, the actual ongoing operational latency in Post-Quantum environments relies upon Encapsulation (Alice securely transmitting symmetric materials) and Decapsulation (Bob recovering the symmetric key).

\begin{figure}[htbp]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{fig3_encaps_time}
\caption{Encapsulation cycle latency across algorithms. Notice the distinct clustering of \kyber{} tightly beneath 10ms thresholds globally.}
\label{fig:encaps}
\end{figure}

\begin{figure}[htbp]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{fig4_decaps_time}
\caption{Decapsulation operations frequently consume slightly elevated computational overhead compared to Encapsulation uniformly.}
\label{fig:decaps}
\end{figure}

As exhibited in Figures \ref{fig:encaps} and \ref{fig:decaps}, lattice-based KEMs (\kyber{} and \ntru{}) exhibit extraordinarily tight encapsulation variances. However, decapsulation consistently draws roughly 15-20\% higher instructional penalties due to the required re-encryption steps embedded within the Fujisaki-Okamoto (FO) transform mandated for achieving strict IND-CCA2 security against active chosen-ciphertext adversaries.

\subsection{Variance and Execution Consistency}

OS-level jitter is unavoidable on non-bare-metal platforms, and its magnitude depends on the algorithm's memory footprint. Figure 10 shows the effect clearly through box plots of execution time.

\begin{figure*}[htbp]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{fig6_boxplot}
\caption{Boxplot distribution highlighting significant jitter anomalies distinctly present in memory-heavy instantiations.}
\label{fig:boxplot}
\end{figure*}

The pattern: algorithms that push closer to the RAM ceiling produce wider IQRs and longer tails. ML-DSA-5 on Class 1 hardware, for instance, exhibits outlier runs exceeding 3$\times$ its median — likely caused by the kernel reclaiming pages when the heap nears the 50 KB limit. For any sensor bound to RTOS deadlines (e.g., a 100 ms polling cycle), these tail events are the real constraint, not the median.

\subsection{Correlation Matrices and Radar Fingerprints}

To analytically prove the independence of our target variables (Energy vs Memory vs Time), Pearson correlation coefficients were generated (Figure \ref{fig:correlation}).

\begin{figure}[htbp]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{fig7_correlation}
\caption{Pearson correlation matrix mapping the deterministic relationship between metric payloads.}
\label{fig:correlation}
\end{figure}

Figure \ref{fig:correlation} reports the Pearson correlation matrix across all measured attributes. Energy and execution time correlate at +0.98 — expected, since $E_{cpu}$ is directly proportional to active cycles. The more informative finding is the weak link between public key size and CPU energy (+0.42). This confirms that computation cost and transmission cost are largely independent: an algorithm can be fast to compute but expensive to transmit (FN-DSA), or cheap to send but slow to execute (SLH-DSA).

\begin{figure*}[htbp]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{fig8_radar}
\caption{Multi-dimensional radar profiles isolating algorithmic strengths across five constraint dimensions simultaneously.}
\label{fig:radar}
\end{figure*}

The radar charts in Figure \ref{fig:radar} make this trade-off visual. FN-DSA-512 collapses the signature-size and verification-time axes to near-zero, but its key-generation axis stretches far beyond every competitor. SLH-DSA-128f shows the opposite shape: a 32-byte public key (the smallest in the set) paired with a 17 KB signature and a 1,110 ms signing time.

\subsection{Latency Cumulative Distribution}

Averages hide tail risk. Figure \ref{fig:cdf} plots the cumulative distribution of execution times across all 45,000 traces.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{fig13_cdf}
\caption{Cumulative Distribution Function (CDF) mapping the probabilistic probability of generating output payload beneath distinct latency bounds.}
\label{fig:cdf}
\end{figure}

The practical takeaway: 95\% of ML-KEM-512 runs finish in under 8 ms, across all device classes. That 8 ms ceiling is what matters for a sensor polling at 10 Hz — it guarantees the crypto never becomes the bottleneck. SLH-DSA-128f, by contrast, does not reach its 95th percentile until well past 1,000 ms, ruling it out for any real-time application.

\subsection{Security Level vs Resource Pareto Frontier}

Every step up in NIST security level costs resources. The question is how steeply. Figure \ref{fig:pareto} plots resource drain (log scale) against security level for each algorithm and device class, revealing the Pareto frontier.

\begin{figure*}[htbp]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{fig14_pareto}
\caption{Pareto Frontier analyzing global Resource Drain multipliers linearly versus NIST security gradations identically.}
\label{fig:pareto}
\end{figure*}

ML-KEM-512 and FN-DSA-512 (with hardware FPU) sit on or near the Pareto curve at Level 1: they deliver 128-bit post-quantum security at the lowest combined cost. Moving to Level 3, ML-KEM-768 stays close to the frontier — a 1.45$\times$ timing increase and 1.50$\times$ memory increase over ML-KEM-512 (Table IX). SLH-DSA variants, by contrast, land orders of magnitude above the frontier at every security level, pushed there by their hash-tree signing cost.

\subsection{Execution vs Bandwidth Tradeoffs}

Finally, Figure \ref{fig:tradeoff} clusters algorithms logically by assessing processor execution intensity directly against the necessitated bandwidth payloads.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{fig11_tradeoff}
\caption{Execution complexity specifically mapped directly against requisite RF transmission overheads dynamically outlining the operational landscape.}

No single PQC algorithm is ideal for IoT. Each demands a tradeoff between RF air-time (bandwidth) and CPU cycle drain (compute). Architects deploying constrained infrastructure must pre-calculate identical constraint profiles mapped tightly against these empirical clusters exactly.
