\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}
%-----------------------------------------------------------------------

RSA and ECC will not survive a sufficiently capable quantum computer. Shor's algorithm \cite{shor1994algorithms} reduces integer factorization and discrete logarithm extraction to polynomial time — a result that invalidates the hardness assumptions underlying virtually every deployed asymmetric primitive. The practical question is not whether this matters, but when. And for systems whose encrypted traffic must remain confidential for decades, 'when' is already too late \cite{mosca2018cybersecurity}. 

In response to this existential-level cryptographic threat, the National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST) concluded its multi-year standardization program on August 13, 2024, which finalized the initial set of critical post-quantum cryptography (PQC) standards: FIPS 203 (ML-KEM), FIPS 204 (ML-DSA), and FIPS 205 (SLH-DSA) \cite{nist2024pqc}. This marks the beginning of the global transition to quantum-resistant cryptography.

IoT is where the post-quantum migration will hurt most. The ecosystem — projected at 75 billion devices by 2025 \cite{statista2021iot} — spans pacemakers, factory floor PLCs, vehicle-to-vehicle radios, and municipal power grids. These are not systems that tolerate a 600 ms cryptographic stall or a 50 KB memory spike. IoT devices operate in tightly constrained environments as defined by RFC 7228 \cite{bormann2014coap}, typically relying on sub-50 MHz microcontrollers and battery lifecycles demanding power budgets under a single milliwatt.

The looming shift from classical RSA/ECC foundations toward lattice and hash-based constructions isn't merely a mathematical update; for IoT architects, it's a fundamental resource collision. While NIST's August 2024 finalization of FIPS 203–205 provided the standard, the operational reality on Class 0–2 hardware remains largely unmapped. Our investigation addresses this by moving beyond simple execution-time metrics to establish a unified energy-drain profile—factoring in both the localized 'compute cost' of matrix arithmetic and the 'radio cost' of transmitting bloated PQC payloads.

\subsection{Novelty and Research Gap}

The pqm4 project \cite{kannwischer2019pqm4} benchmarked Cortex-M4 assembly. Fitzgibbon and Ottaviani \cite{fitzgibbon2024benchmarking} extended this to Raspberry Pi Linux targets. Silva et al. \cite{secrypt2025esp32} tested ESP32 under FreeRTOS. Each study profiled a subset of algorithms on a single hardware tier. None mapped the full algorithm suite — ML-KEM, ML-DSA, FN-DSA, SLH-DSA, and NTRU — across all three RFC 7228 classes simultaneously, and none combined execution profiling with RF energy modeling or formal multi-criteria ranking.

With the latest standardized algorithms defined by NIST, what schemes are viable for specific classes of IoT devices, and what is the relative cost to classical cryptography, and what are the trade-offs?

\subsection{Contributions}

Our contributions address this collision directly:
\begin{enumerate}
 \item \textbf{Comprehensive Cross-Class Benchmarking:} We ran 45,000+ measurement iterations across 14 parameter configurations, enforcing strict C0/C1/C2 memory ceilings via OS-level resource limits to simulate genuine RFC 7228 hardware.
 \item \textbf{Expanded Energy and Communication Analysis:} We propose a unified energy estimation model that accounts for microcontroller cycle drain and radio frequency transmission overheads of inflated PQC key sizes.
 \item \textbf{TOPSIS Decision Framework:} We apply a multi-attribute statistical decision matrix to objectively evaluate and classify algorithms in terms of speed, memory footprint, energy, key size, and security scale to yield the first deterministic PQC algorithm deployment guide targeting IoT system architects.
 \item \textbf{Classical vs. Quantum and Hybrid Profiling:} Classical bounds are established using RSA and ECC algorithms to determine migration costs, including overhead metrics of Hybrid TLS 1.3 models to bridge the classical-quantum divide.
\end{enumerate}
